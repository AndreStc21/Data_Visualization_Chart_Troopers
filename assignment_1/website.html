CO2 emissions initial analysis

Comparing countries, regions and emissions type

In this section, we present an initial analysis of global CO2 emissions, providing an overview of emissions by country and region, both in total and per capita. Additionally, we include an analysis of emissions by type for specific countries.

Top 10 - 2022 per capita CO2 emissions

To read the histogram, look at the horizontal axis for the countries and the vertical axis for CO2 emissions per capita in tonnes. The height of each bar shows the emissions level for each country.

Per capita emissions can be misleading indicators, as in our case, especially when countries have very small, wealthy populations that skew the overall assessment.
However, it remains an important metric because it highlights that the wealthiest individuals often pollute significantly more than the average person.
This metric shifts the focus from identifying the most polluting countries to pinpointing the individuals and groups with the highest environmental impact.

Top 10 - 2022-2012 per capita CO2 emissions

Again, to read the histogram, look at the horizontal axis for the countries and the vertical axis for CO2 emissions per capita in tonnes. The height of each bar shows the emissions level for each country.

We see that wealthy countries tend to have higher per capita emissions due to higher living standards, greater consumption of goods and services, energy-intensive industries, and reliance on fossil fuels for energy.
With smaller populations, even high emissions from specific sectors can result in a high per capita average.

Top 5 - 2022 total CO2 emissions - Continental comparison

For each continent, countries are sorted by the most polluter to the 5th most polluter, the last rectangle instead represents the total emissions of all the other countries in the same continent.

Asian countries have the highest emissions, followed by Europe and North America, whose combined emissions do not match Asia's total.
Asia's emission have these high levels probably because they have a huge population.

Top 5 - 2022 total CO2 emissions

Each column represents the CO2 emissions of the five most polluting countries in each region, with the final column showing the cumulative emissions of all other countries in that region.

Asian countries, despite their large populations, stand out for their exceptionally high CO2 emissions, with China emitting more than double the amount of the USA.
This highlights Asia's significant contribution to global emissions.
In contrast, Africa and Oceania show much lower total emissions, reflecting smaller industrial activities and economies. The final plot emphasizes that even when individual countries in a region contribute less, their combined emissions can still represent a notable share of the total.

For each continent, countries are sorted by the most polluter to the 5th most polluter, the last rectangle instead represents the total emissions of all the other countries in the same continent.

Clearly, richer countries pollute more, and it is easy to notice since the first country for each continent is the richest one!

Top 10 - 2022 CO2 emission types comparison - Fossil vs Land

The heatmaps represent CO2 emissions split into two categories: fossil fuel emissions (top row) and land-use emissions (bottom row).

- Shades of red indicate less emissions, while darker red represent higher emissions.
- Shades of green indicate negative emissions (net CO2 absorption), with darker green representing stronger absorption.
- White signifies near-zero emissions or absorption.

China clearly stands out as the largest emitter in both fossil and land-use categories, particularly in fossil emissions.
The United States and India follow as major contributors in fossil emissions.
Interestingly, countries like Indonesia and China show significant land-use emissions, which reflect deforestation and land-use changes. On the other hand, Iran, Germany and others show negligible or no significant land-use emissions, focusing their impact on fossil fuel consumption. This dual perspective highlights the varying nature of CO2 contributions, where some nations contribute predominantly through industrial activities, while others are more impacted by land-use practices.
